Data Interpretation

Tutor: Mary

We will be working on interpreting scatterplots today!

The figure on your right is an example of a Scatterplot.

In a scatter plot, the values of one variable appear on the horizontal axis of a rectangular coordinate system and the values of the other variable appear on the vertical axis.

For each individual or object in the data, an ordered pair of numbers is collected, one number for each variable, and the pair is represented by a point in the coordinate system.

This scatterplot shows how the finishing time for a certain bike race was related to the amount of physical training in the 3 months leading up to the race.

For example, one point corresponds to about 32 units in Training Index and 3.55 hours in Finishing Time.

Data Interpretation

Tutor: Mary

Let’s look at the context of this figure to get a better picture of what is plotted here!

This scatterplot pertains to a study where a bicycle trainer studied how the finishing time for a certain bike race was related to the amount of physical training in the 3 months leading up to the race.

To measure the amount of training, the trainer developed a training index based on the intensity of each bicyclist’s training, measured in “units.” In total, the trainer examined the training index and finishing time of 50 bicyclists.

Therefore, each point in the plot represents data of one bicyclist. The point we looked at previously represented a bicyclist who had approximately 32 units of training and finished the race in about 3.55 hours.

Data Interpretation

Tutor: Mary

Now let’s look at the trend line.

A scatterplot makes it possible to observe an overall pattern, or trend, in the relationship between the two variables. In this case, we observe the relationship between Training Index and Finishing Time.

The trend line demonstrates how scattered, or close the data are to the trend line, or to put it another way, how well the trend line fits the data. In our scatterplot, almost all of the data points are close to the trend line.

The trend line shows that the finishing times generally decrease as the training indices increase; it can be written as “vertical variable = slope * horizontal variable + intercept(the value of vertical variable when the horizontal variable has a value of 0).”

With the trend line, we can make predictions about a bicyclist’s finishing time given the training index.

Data Interpretation

Tutor: Mary

Let's take a look at a sample GRE question!

Question: According to the trend line, if the difference in the training index of two of the bicyclists was 20 units, what is the predicted difference between finishing time of the two bicyclists?

In order to make the prediction, we must first calculate the slope of the trend line. We see that the trend line passes very close to the points (30, 5.0) and (50, 4.5). What do you think the slope of the trend line is given these two points? Hint: Slope = (Y1 - Y2)/(X1 - X2)

0.025

The slope is: (4.5-5.0)/(50-30) = -0.5/20 = -0.025

The slope tells us that for every 1 unit increase in training index, the trend line predicts a decrease in finishing time of approximately 0.025 hours.

Data Interpretation

Tutor: James

I will be walking you through circle graphs today!

On your right is an example of circle graphs, or pie charts. This type of graph is commonly used to represent data with a relatively small number of categories.

They illustrate how a whole is separated into parts. The data is represented in a circle such that the area of the circle representing each category is proportional to the part of the whole that the category represents.

Data Interpretation

Tutor: James

Some more details

For example, the pie charts on your right demonstrate 1) the workforce distribution by occupational category for Region Y in 2001, and 2) the projected workforce distribution by occupational category in the same region in 2015.

In 2015, the pie chart shows that Manufacturing was 20% of the workforce. This means that the area occupied by manufacturing in the pie is 20% of the total area of the pie, or 35 million people.

Each part of a circle graph is called a sector. Because the area of each sector is proportional to the percent of the whole that the sector represents, the measure of the central angle of a sector is proportional to the percent of 360 degrees that the sector represents.
